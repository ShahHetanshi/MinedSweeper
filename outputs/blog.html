Unintended PCR Products from Mismatched Primers: The Impact of Nucleotide Loop-Outs and Magnesium Ion Concentration

Summary Background: Mismatched primers in PCR (polymerase chain reaction) can be used to create restriction enzyme recognition sites for mutation detection. Methods and Results: The authors designed a PCR assay to detect the T3920~A mutation in the APC gene using a mismatched primer. However, the PCR product did not digest completely with the restriction enzyme Mse I, indicating an unexpected result. Sequence analysis revealed that the T3920~A mutation was not present. Instead, a single nucleotide loop-out in the genomic DNA template caused the 3' terminal base of the primer to hybridize incorrectly, creating a modified product. At a lower magnesium ion concentration, the intended product was produced. Conclusions: Mismatched primers can produce unintended PCR products due to nucleotide loop-outs. Magnesium ion concentration can influence the sequence and amount of the product. Key Concepts: APC gene: Gene associated with colorectal cancer. DNA diagnosis: Identifying mutations or variations in DNA for diagnostic purposes. Mismatched primer: Primer designed with an intentional mismatch to create a restriction enzyme site.
